Albania

Bunkers, Brutalism, and Balkan Peaks

My days of relaxation on the beaches of Montenegro had come to an end, for it was time to (once again) put my faith in Balkan bus drivers and weave through the mountains. I cross the southern border and head for Shkodër, a small city in northern Albania.

Shkodër is the base for all mountain excursions in northern Albania, the beginning of the famed 'Peaks of the Balkans' trail. My hostel was able to provide a rough itinerary, however it proved to be quite convoluted. I was given a series of interconnected guest houses along my 3 day trek, as well as the various versions of transportation required to reach my starting point. With no confirmation of availability, I decided to take my chances.

Reaching the first guesthouse in the town of Valbonë required a trip up Komani Lake, a narrow fjord that descends from the peaks.

After a full day of travel, I arrive in Valbonë for sunset.

The Peaks of the Balkans trail winds through remote, mountainous regions of the Western Balkans. Only in the past decade has this trail become accessible, as it traverses Albania, Kosovo, and Montenegro in a circular loop.

The Albanian section offers fast elevation changes, freezing streams, and homemade chai.

15km southwest of Valbonë lies the mountain village of Theth. After 7 hours of walking, I descend into this hidden valley.

After a warm Tavë Kosi and some rest, I pack up and head out to the Blue Eye Canyon, just 10km south of Theth.

With my trek coming to an end, I catch the mountain bus from Theth to Shkodër, then to Albania's capital, Tirana.

Tirana in the 20th century saw a kaleidoscope of change, transitioning from a quiet Ottoman town under monarchist rule to a repressive, hermit kingdom run by a paranoid, communist dictator. Today, Tirana is known for its brutalist architecture and vast quantities of bunkers.

Unfortunately, my stay in Tirana was quite short. It was once again time to catch the local bus, heading east towards North Macedonia.

France

Hauts-de-France and Paris

In the northeast of France, tucked against the Belgian border, lies Lille - a small university city unique for its blend of French and Flemish influence.
After a short 1 hour train, I arrive at the port town of Dunkerque, famous for the 1940 allied retreat.
After 5 days in Hauts-de-France, I travel to the world-renouned city of Paris. In the 1st arrondissement of Paris, in the heart of Île de la Cité, lies Sainte-Chapelle. Originally completed in 1248, this gothic cathedral contains 15 glass walls, all 15 metres tall. A key feature of Sainte-Chapelle is the rose window on the south side. The window depicts the Book of Revelation — the final section of the Bible with a prophecy on the end of the current age. Located in the outskirts of Paris is the Palace of Versailles, a symbol of absolutism during the rule of the "sun king" Louis XIV who reigned from 1643 until his death in 1715. Louis centralized power in the monarchy through the Palace of Versailles, consolidating authority and diminishing the influence of the nobility. His famous declaration - "L'État, c'est moi" - epitomizes his belief in the divine right of kings and absolute rule. A key feature of the palace is the sprawling 8 square kilometre garden network. The grandeur of the gardens complements the opulence of the palace, creating a seamless integration of architecture and nature. Follow along with my journey, heading north into the Netherlands.
